# Author Style Summaries

---

## The Night Land — William Hope Hodgson

The Night Land is the most extreme work in the 141-work corpus, and its strangeness is syntactic, not ornamental. Its single biggest anomaly is the function word **"be" (z = +7.6)**, the largest deviation of any series on any work — driven by Hodgson's invented auxiliary construction *"did be"* (1,552 occurrences). The connective scaffolding follows: *that* (+5.0), *so* (+4.7), *to* (+3.7), *and* (+3.3); 1,785 paragraphs open with "And," and 86% of sentences are complex. Its **archaic-word rate (z = +5.0)** ranks #2 of 141, behind only *The Worm Ouroboros* (*unto* ×957, *doth* ×226, *in verity* ×189). Yet its **mean word length is the lowest in the entire corpus
(z = −3.2)** — the paradox at the style's heart: the vocabulary is plain, short, Anglo-Saxon; the archaism lives entirely in grammar. Sentences average 39 words (#2) with the corpus's second-highest length variance; semicolons run at +4.0z; contractions: **zero** in 198k words. High Yule's K with low Honoré's R means a small vocabulary hammered repetitively — incantation, not decoration. Even against Hodgson's own eight other works, "be" sits +6.9z above his norm.

## The Worm Ouroboros — E. R. Eddison

Ouroboros's signature is **archaism at outlier-of-outliers scale**: 2.24% of its words are archaic — **z = +6.61**, first in the 141-work corpus at nearly eight times the average, far ahead of runner-up *The Night Land* (1.76%). This isn't decorative "thee/thou" dusting; it's systemic Jacobean-Norse grammar: *wrastling, betwixt, whenas, spake, gat, withal, yesternight*. Second: **rhythm through variance, not length**. Mean sentence length is only mildly high (23.0 words, z = +0.49), but sentence-length standard deviation is 17.1 (**z = +1.93**, 98th percentile) — massive Homeric periods alternating with abrupt saga exchanges ("The King said, 'I swear.'"). Third: **saga architecture**. Dialogue-to-narration ratio (0.47) is more than double the corpus norm (+1.2z); *so* (+2.5) and *and* (+1.3) chain clauses paratactically, while *had, been, would* run 1.3–1.7σ low — the story lives in immediate preterite, shunning past-perfect and conditional hedging. Finally, the diction is **short and Anglo-Saxon**, not Latinate: mean word length 4.22 (z = −1.16) despite rich vocabulary (low Yule's K); colons run high (+1.1z), ellipses are literally zero, dashes and contractions near-absent. The measurable fingerprint of a 1600s saga translation, published in 1922.

## Titus Groan — Mervyn Peake

What sets Titus Groan apart is sentence *rhythm*, not vocabulary. Its sentence-length standard deviation is 16.3 words (**z = +1.66**, 8th of 141) against a merely average mean of 17.7 (z = −0.22); the spread-to-mean ratio (0.92, 5th in the corpus, with *Gormenghast* 3rd) is the Peake signature — enormous winding architectural sentences set against abrupt short ones, rather than uniformly long prose. Equally telling is what *doesn't* register: in a corpus stacked with ornate stylists (Smith, Eddison, Hodgson), Peake's famous lushness isn't lexical. Archaic-word rate is average (z = −0.15), mean word length low (z = −0.80), adjective density *below* average (z = −0.67), semicolons low (z = −0.85), hapax richness modest (Honoré's R, z = −0.81). The ornate effect is built from syntax and imagery, not rare words or adjective-stacking. Function words expose the method: **"at" (z = +1.66**, 6th) marks relentlessly spatial, locative narration; "her" (+1.59) the female-heavy cast; and strikingly scarce "if" (**z = −1.43**, 129th) a prose that declares rather than speculates. Dialogue sits at 17.8% of words, modestly under the 22% corpus mean (z = −0.20) — narration leads, and the talk (Prunesquallor, Slagg) skews interrogative: question marks run at +1.1z.
